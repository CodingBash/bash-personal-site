Hello!



My name is Basheer Becerra (pronounced as BUH-sheer bi-SE-ruh)!

I am currently an first year PhD Candidate in Bioinformatics and Integrative Genomics (BIG) attending Harvard Medical School. I recently graduated from Illinois State University with a double major in computer science and statistics and a biology minor.

RESEARCH INTERESTS: I am interested in hypothesis-driven computational biology to understand the underlying mechanisms of human disease with a focus on cancer, aging, and rare genetic disorders. Additionally, I am interested in developing computational/statistical methods and tools to support the aforementioned research interest.

PERSONAL STORY: Thinking back to my past, I'm extraordinarily grateful and blessed for making it to where I am right now, a state of serenity and also working towards a purpose so aligned with my core. I grew up in a small rural town of El Paso in central Illinois, a very peaceful area with a small population of around 2000 people and surrounded by cornfields. Throughout my childhood, I focused on athletics, specifically soccer, so that one day I could hopefully earn a scholarship to play in college - at the time, academics was not really a priority nor did I ever think it would be. I was always a typical/average student. Despite playing in several soccer teams and earning the attention of college recruiters, I decided to leave soccer before my senior year of high school to find something of greater motivation that I truly wanted to do rather than being forced by circumstance. Coincidentally at the time, I took a computer science course near the end of high school that was likely my favorite class I've taken. I spent most of my free time (which I had a lot of since I left all my soccer teams) on learning how to code simply for pure enjoyment, and I quickly developed my computer science skills. After being accepted to State Farm's IT internship the summer after high school graduation and meeting other like-minded students, computer science became a field that I wanted to pursue throughout my career. Since I had no chance of being recruited to college for soccer, I enrolled at local college 15 minutes away from my hometown, Illinois State University, to study computer science. Many college students discover another passion during the beginning of college - for me, that was biology (due to pure fascination of life). During my sophomore year, I integrated these two interests to spark a newfound interest in computational biology. After a bit of experience, I knew that this was the primary field I wanted to pursue. Even though my university didn't provide a formal program for computational biology, I independently forged my own path by working with biology and computer science professors, independently learning relevant material, taking as many courses in each of the sub-disciplines of computational biology, and pursuing research opportunities and internships. Despite several challenges along the way, my desire to explore a field I enjoyed manifested into a purpose to help others through fundamental and translational research. Now that I am studying bioinformatics at Harvard Medical School and training to perform research to oneday help others, I find it incredible how staying true to one's own pure interests and desires can take oneself to great lengths. This inspires me to inspire society to be fully actualized - while also striving to be the best version of myself academically, athletically, and as a human being.

Illinois State University
Bioinformatics Undergraduate Researcher - January 2017 – Present

Principal Investigator: Dr. Alysia Vrailas-Mortimer
Project Title: “Proteomic Analysis of p38 in Drosophila melanogaster.”
I independently performed proteomic analysis of differentially expressed proteins in short-lived p38K knockout, long-lived p38K over-expression, and wild-type Drosophila melanogaster brains and muscles to discover mechanisms in aging and age-dependent diseases. Furthermore, I independently developed a statistical-based technique that estimates the biological age of Drosophila melanogaster mutants using wild-type protein expression profiles.

Principal Investigator: Dr. Nathan T. Mortimer
Project Title: “FlyCAGE: Correlational Analysis of Gene Expression in Drosophila melanogaster.”
I independently developed a bioinformatics web tool that conducts analysis of Drosophila melanogaster (fruit fly) mRNA expression data to discover genes with correlated spatiotemporal expression.

Cold Spring Harbor Laboratory
Quantitative Biology Undergraduate Research Program Participant - June 2018 – August 2018

Principal Investigator: Dr. Alexander Krasnitz
Project Title: “Copy Number Analysis (CNA) of Pancreatic Cancer Organoids to Predict Drug Response.”
I performed genomic profiling of organoids derived from patients with pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC). I developed a method using statistical learning and other pre-processing algorithms to predict chemotherapeutic drug response using extracted copy-number features. This analysis was also used to characterize molecular subtypes of PDAC based on their extracted copy-number features.
